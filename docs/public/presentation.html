[1 minute] First, why care? Drug discovery is slow. Bringing a single drug to market costs around two billion dollars and takes over ten years. And even after all that investment, about ninety percent of drug candidates fail somewhere in clinical trials. A huge part of that cost is figuring out what a molecule actually does to a living cell — does it hit the target you think it's hitting? Does it cause side effects? Today, answering that question requires expensive assays, specialized expertise, and a lot of trial and error. So the question we're interested in is: can we get a much faster, cheaper way to ask "what does this molecule actually do to a cell?" And that's where cell imaging comes in — which is the next slide.

[1 minute] Cell Painting is a clever microscopy technique that gives us a rich visual fingerprint of what's happening inside a cell. We stain the cell with five different fluorescent dyes — each highlighting a different structure: DNA, the endoplasmic reticulum, RNA and nucleoli, the actin cytoskeleton and Golgi, and mitochondria. Then we take a photo in each color channel. Put them together and you get an incredibly detailed snapshot — essentially a five-channel photograph that captures the state of the cell. Now the key part: when you apply a perturbation — a drug compound, a CRISPR gene knockout, or overexpressing a gene — the cell changes. Sometimes dramatically, sometimes very subtly. But those changes show up in the images. A trained biologist can often tell what family of drug was applied just by looking. The question is: can an AI learn to do the same?

[1 minute] Our approach is inspired by a model many of you may have heard of: CLIP, released by OpenAI in 2021. CLIP learned to connect everyday photos with their text captions — which is why you can now say things like "show me photos of a sunset over mountains" and get meaningful results. It learned a shared language between pictures and words. MorphoCLIP takes that same idea and applies it to biology. Instead of sunsets and mountains, we work with cell microscopy images. Instead of casual captions, we use biological descriptions — like "a compound that inhibits tubulin and disrupts cell division." The goal is to place matching image-text pairs at the same point in a shared embedding space. Once that works, we can search, compare, and cluster perturbations using plain language. A biologist could literally ask the system "find me cells showing DNA damage stress" and get back relevant examples — no feature engineering required. Now let's look at how this actually works under the hood.

How It Works

Two encoders learn to place matching image + text pairs at the same point in a shared space.

[1.5 minutes] Here's the architecture at a glance. Two parallel branches, both feeding into a shared understanding space on the right. On top: we take the five-channel cell image and pass it through what I'm calling the Vision AI — that's DINOv3, a powerful pretrained vision model from Meta. We keep it frozen, meaning we don't retrain its weights; we just use its existing understanding of visual patterns. On the bottom: we take a biological description in plain English and pass it through BioClinical BERT — a language model that was pretrained on biomedical text. Also frozen. Both branches produce vectors — lists of numbers — that live in the same shared space. And during training, we do one simple thing: when an image and its correct description come in as a pair, we pull their vectors together. When they don't match, we push them apart. Over time, that shared space organizes itself so that similar biology — say, all tubulin inhibitors, or all DNA damage agents — ends up clustered together, regardless of whether you arrived there from an image or a sentence. That's the whole trick. And because both encoders are frozen, we only train small adapter layers on top — which means we can iterate fast on a single GPU.

[45 seconds] Quick note on our data. We're working with CPJUMP1 — a pilot dataset from the JUMP Cell Painting Consortium. It's 56 plates of cell images, covering all three perturbation types: small molecules, CRISPR knockouts, and ORF overexpressions. And importantly, it's fully public on AWS S3 through the Cell Painting Gallery. That means anyone can reproduce our results, and everything we build on top is shareable. This dataset was designed by the consortium specifically to be a clean benchmark for cross-modal learning methods like ours — so it's the right starting point.

Why It's Hard

Batch effects

Same compound looks different across plates, days, and microscopes — noise can swamp the biological signal.

Fuzzy ground truth

Drugs are rarely strictly "same" or "different" — they share some targets but not others. No clean labels.

Scale + compute

Millions of images. We pre-cache features so training runs on a single RTX 5080 in minutes per epoch.

Our answers: CWCL soft-contrastive loss · cross-well batch alignment · pre-computed DINOv3 + BERT features

[1 minute] Now, why isn't this already solved? Three real challenges. First: batch effects. The same compound applied to cells on Monday versus Wednesday, or on two different microscopes, can produce visually different images. That technical noise can completely swamp the biological signal we're trying to learn. Second: fuzzy ground truth. Unlike cats and dogs, drugs don't fall into clean categories. Two compounds might share one molecular target but not another. So there's no simple "positive" or "negative" label — it's a spectrum. Third: sheer scale. We're talking millions of images. To train efficiently, we pre-compute and cache features from both encoders once, so each training epoch runs in minutes on a single RTX 5080. Our technical answers — you don't need to memorize these — are CWCL, a soft-contrastive loss that handles the fuzzy labels, cross-well alignment for batch correction, and pre-computed features for speed.

[45 seconds] Before I show you numbers, who are we comparing against? CellProfiler is the classical incumbent — for over a decade it's been the go-to tool for extracting handcrafted features from cell images: shape, texture, intensity. It's widely used but limited by what humans thought to measure. Chandrasekaran 2024 is the reference paper that defines the CPJUMP1 benchmark itself, published in Nature Methods. CellCLIP is the closest recent peer — a contrastive model like ours, but trained only on chemical compounds. MorphoCLIP is the only approach here that handles all three perturbation types — compounds, CRISPR, and ORFs — and connects them to natural-language descriptions. We measure retrieval accuracy: given an image, can the model pick out the right text description from hundreds of candidates? And vice versa.

[1 minute] Here's what we're seeing on the validation split — about 2,200 wells with 817 candidate perturbations. Random guessing would give you a top-1 accuracy of around 0.12%. Left chart shows recall at k — the percentage of queries where the correct answer appears in the top k results. Look at text-to-image, the purple bars. Recall at 10 is 24.3% — meaning when we describe a perturbation in text, the correct cell image appears in the top ten results about a quarter of the time. That's roughly 200 times better than random. Image-to-text is harder, and there's an intuitive reason. Many different cell images share the same text label — so even when the model returns a reasonable answer, it may not be the exact label we marked as "correct." You can see this on the right chart: for text-to-image, the median rank is just 28 out of 817. That means the correct perturbation ends up in the top three and a half percent on average. Image-to-text is harder, at median rank 289, but still dramatically above the random baseline shown in red. The punchline: the model genuinely learned biology. It's not memorizing — it's making meaningful connections between what cells look like and what they mean.

[45 seconds] How does that stack up against prior work? Let me be upfront: these three approaches each tackle slightly different tasks, so this isn't a strict apples-to-apples comparison. But the numbers are in the same regime and the comparison is informative. CellProfiler, the classical method — when asked to find sister perturbations that target the same gene — achieves a fraction retrieved somewhere between 4% and 25% depending on the setting, averaging around 13%. CellCLIP, on the Bray dataset which is its reported benchmark, hits recall at 10 of about 7.4% in both directions. MorphoCLIP, on CPJUMP1, reaches recall at 10 of 24.3% in the text-to-image direction. That's roughly three times CellCLIP's best number, on a larger candidate pool, and well above CellProfiler's handcrafted-feature range. Plus — and this is a structural advantage — MorphoCLIP handles all three perturbation types. CellCLIP is compound-only. CellProfiler has no text interface at all.

Why It Matters

Drug repurposing

Find existing drugs whose cellular signature matches a new target — far cheaper than starting from scratch.

Mechanism discovery

Unknown compound lands near "tubulin inhibitors" in embedding space → strong hypothesis about how it works.

Natural-language screens

Biologists query in English: "cells showing mitochondrial stress" — no feature engineering required.

[1 minute] So what does all this actually buy us in the real world? Drug repurposing. If a new disease has a known cellular signature, we can search across thousands of existing, already-approved drugs and ask which ones cause cells to look similar. That could identify candidates in days rather than the years and billions we mentioned at the start. Mechanism discovery. Imagine a researcher has a new compound they don't fully understand. They pass its cell image through MorphoCLIP, and it lands right next to known tubulin inhibitors in the embedding space. That's an immediate, testable hypothesis — it probably disrupts microtubules. And natural-language screens. Biologists don't have to write code, define features, or train classifiers. They can literally ask the system, in English — "show me cells with mitochondrial stress" — and get back relevant examples from the dataset. These aren't hypothetical — these are the workflows that pharma, academic labs, and platforms like the JUMP consortium want. MorphoCLIP is one step toward making them practical.

Future Plan

Scale up the data

Move beyond the CPJUMP1 pilot to the full JUMP dataset — ~140k perturbations across many sources and cell lines.

Richer prompts

Pull pathway and mechanism-of-action descriptions from UniProt, Reactome, and DrugBank for more biological signal.

Zero-shot transfer

Evaluate on unseen Cell Painting datasets (different labs, microscopes, cell types) to measure true generalization.

Open benchmark + demo

Release the model, evaluation suite, and an interactive natural-language retrieval demo for the community.

[1 minute] Where do we go from here? Four directions — these are complementary, not strictly sequential. We'll prioritize based on what the results tell us. First: scale up the data. CPJUMP1 is a pilot — the full JUMP dataset has around 140,000 perturbations across many different labs, microscopes, and cell lines. That's a much harder test and a much more useful model. Second: richer prompts. Right now our text descriptions are relatively simple. We want to pull in pathway-level and mechanism-of-action information from databases like UniProt, Reactome, and DrugBank — giving the text encoder more biological signal to work with. Third: zero-shot transfer. The real test of a representation is: does it work on data it's never seen before? We want to evaluate on completely unseen Cell Painting datasets — different labs, different cell types — to measure true generalization. And fourth: open benchmark and demo. We plan to release the model, the evaluation suite, and an interactive natural-language retrieval demo, so the broader community can build on this work.
